  1. What would Tufte say about this data graphic? Critique it from his point-of-view.

He would say that ink is wasted in printing the order from 1 to 20 over and over again on the lines. You can start at line 1 and count down yourself to get the ordering. You only need markings in which there is something different about the ordering. In the case of this graphic, there is a rank 24 and the tie for rank 5 that needs to be noted. Also, the change in rank is encoded in both the color and direction of the line. Color can be removed without any loss of information. I wonder what is the point of putting in the country the university is located in? Location is semicoded in the university’s name. Is the graphic trying to make a point about US vs UK universities? Or making a point about the top rankings of universities being dominated by the UK and US? Also, this graphic relies primarily on reds and greens. Red-green color blind people would have difficulty viewing this graphic.

In the “What is a Data Scientist?” set of graphics, each of the graphics compare percentages.

All of the graphics share this black/blue/gray color scheme. However, from graphic to graphic each color means something very different. It gets confusing changing between the graphics and having to change your definition of what blue means.

One graphic uses shades of blue. The darker shade means a higher percentage while lighter shades represent lower percentages. It’s difficult to differentiate between the shades of blue and thus it’s difficult to visualize the differences between the percentages.

With the pie graphs, it is difficult to tell that one portion is bigger than another without the explicit percentage labels. Some of the graphics use bar area to represent the percentages. In “The Source of New Data Science” the 24% bar looks like it can fit 4 of the 12% bar. The 12% bar looks like it can fit 20 of the 2% bar.

Another graphic uses the areas of circles to represent percentages, but the areas look exaggerated compared to the percentages. The 1% circle looks like it can fit into the 5% circle at least 10 times.

Another graphic uses bars, and even though each bar represent sa different percentage, they are all the same size.

The biggest downfall of this set of graphics is inconsistency. I would probably choose bars of the same width stacked up on top of each other for each graphic with each full stack representing 100%. The length of the bars would represent the percentage, the portion of 100%. For color, I’d choose it so that the bar with the largest percentage would be one color across all of the stacks. The next largest percentage would be another color across all of the stacks, and so on.
